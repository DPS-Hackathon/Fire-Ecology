Case Study 1: Fighting fire with leaves

Forest fires are a nightmare and result in ecological disasters that are impossible to control through conventional methods. I realised we are fighting a war with the age-old conventional methods of dousing fires by beating them with bushes and tree branches." Vikas tells The Better India.


During his research to stop Jharkhand's horrendous forest fires, Vikas Ujjwal, Divisional Forest Officer of Jharkhand's Lohardaga district, came across a Class 6 physics chapter. According to the 'Fire Triangle' chapter, forest fires can be attributed to three components â€” fuel, heat and oxygen. Considering that there is possibly no way of tackling heat and oxygen, Vikas focussed on the 'fuel' aspect, which in Lohardaga's case was heaps of fallen leaves.

With one stroke, Vikas addressed the village's fire and unemployment issues. He installed a machine to convert dry leaves into briquettes, an eco-friendly artificial fuelwood. This innovative project began in April 2019 and has borne remarkable results. It also aligns with the National Action Plan on Forest Fire, which provides a forest floor biomass management framework. Since its inception, the forests of Jharkhand's Tisiya region have remained free from major fires.

What's even more remarkable is the ripple effect that followed. The briquettes became a potent solution for fire prevention and economic prosperity. Villagers have discontinued the illegal cutting of wood and stopped burning leaves, which are now an asset. The villagers were initially sceptical about whether selling leaves could generate income, but ecotourism success stories later inspired them. Vikas assured them of a guaranteed income with a step-by-step process.


A Joint Forest Management Committee (JFMC) comprising 15 people from different villages oversees the entire briquette plant. The plant can process 1 tonne of leaves daily and employs 400 people from 13 villages.

The initiative has reduced fire by 80% and has developed a source of income for the villagers by selling the briquettes in the local market. In response to the transformation, villagers have ardently requested more of these plants. Vikas, buoyed by their enthusiasm, has promised to fulfil their requests. Lohardaga's forest department and its people represent how ecological preservation and economic growth can go hand-in-hand, setting an inspiring precedent for others.

This case study highlights how a scientific principle and an innovative approach can lead to holistic transformation, promoting sustainable forest management and improving livelihoods.

Case Study 2: Fire and Development: Friends of Foe?

The relationship between fire and development is a multifaceted and intricate one, often presenting challenges and opportunities at the intersection of human progress and ecological dynamics. In the context of urban planning and infrastructure expansion, the consequences of uncontrolled fires can be devastating. One illustrative case study is the Great Fire of London in 1666, which razed a significant portion of the city, leading to the realization of the importance of fire-resistant building materials and better urban design. This catastrophe prompted the adoption of regulations for wider streets and brick construction, laying the foundation for safer urban development.

Conversely, controlled and intentional fire use has been integral to certain developmental practices. Indigenous cultures, particularly in Australia, have employed "fire-stick farming" for millennia. By strategically lighting fires to manage vegetation and promote fresh growth, they not only sustained their livelihoods but also maintained healthy landscapes. This practice underscores the synergy between fire knowledge and sustainable development, emphasizing the need for culturally sensitive approaches that integrate traditional wisdom with modern strategies.

In contemporary times, the relationship between fire and development remains complex. Urban sprawl into fire-prone areas can elevate the risk of wildfires and pose significant challenges for firefighting efforts. The 2018 Camp Fire in California stands as a stark reminder of this, as rapid urban expansion encroached upon areas susceptible to fire. This tragedy underscored the necessity of stringent land-use planning, early warning systems, and community engagement in fire-prone regions.

Nonetheless, controlled burns and prescribed fires are increasingly recognized as essential tools for ecosystem management and development. These practices not only reduce the risk of catastrophic wildfires but also promote forest health and biodiversity. In regions like Australia and the United States, controlled burns are utilized to mimic natural fire regimes, clear out accumulated fuels, and create firebreaks that protect communities and infrastructure.
In conclusion, the intricate relationship between fire and development encapsulates both challenges and opportunities. By drawing lessons from historical catastrophes, incorporating indigenous fire practices, and embracing controlled burn techniques, societies can navigate the complexities of development while fostering resilience against the ever-present force of fire. The key lies in forging a harmonious balance that safeguards lives, infrastructure, and ecosystems while harnessing the regenerative power of fire.

Case Study 3: Fire vs Animals

The intricate dance between fire and animal habitats unfolds as a captivating narrative, showcasing the delicate balance between destruction and rejuvenation, challenge and adaptation. The Yellowstone National Park in the United States serves as a compelling case study, exemplifying how fire's touch can shape and reshape animal landscapes. In 1988, a series of wildfires swept through the park, leaving in their wake a landscape of charred trees and scorched earth. Initially, these scenes painted a picture of devastation, and concerns arose about the well-being of the park's diverse animal inhabitants. However, over time, a remarkable transformation took place. The once-burnt areas, though seemingly desolate, soon burgeoned with new life. Young, succulent vegetation sprouted, nourished by the nutrient-rich ash left behind by the fires. This reawakening of plant life, in turn, attracted herbivores seeking fresh forage. Elk and bison, among other grazers, flocked to the rejuvenated patches, their populations swelling in response to the abundance of food. The cascade continued as predators, such as wolves and bears, followed the herbivores, capitalizing on the revitalized prey base.

Interestingly, the rejuvenation wasn't universal; certain animal species faced challenges due to the changing landscape. Birds that relied on snags (standing dead trees) for nesting were impacted, as many snags were consumed by the fires. However, woodpeckers, adapted to exploiting burnt trees for insects and nest cavities, flourished in the aftermath. This exemplifies the intricate web of interactions in fire, where some species benefit from disturbance while others face setbacks.
Importantly, fire's influence isn't limited to immediate post-fire effects. Over the long term, fire-driven changes can create a mosaic of habitats, each at a different stage of succession. This diversity supports a variety of species, enhancing biodiversity and strengthening ecosystem resilience. The fires of Yellowstone taught ecologists and wildlife managers valuable lessons about the role of fire in shaping animal habitats. Subsequent management strategies now incorporate controlled burns to mimic natural processes, promote vegetation diversity, and create a dynamic landscape that caters to the needs of a wide array of animal species.
In conclusion, the story of fire and its impact on animal habitats is one of complexity and adaptation. Through destruction and rebirth, fire shapes landscapes, influences animal behaviors, and ultimately contributes to the intricate tapestry of life. Recognizing fire's role as a transformative force can guide informed conservation and management decisions, ensuring that animals find harmony amid the dualities of fire's havoc and rejuvenation.
